What Is Mass?

In physics, mass is a fundamental property of matter that measures an object's inertia, or resistance to changes in its state of motion. It is the "amount of stuff" in an object and remains constant regardless of its location or the gravitational field it is in, unlike weight, which is a force that varies with gravity. The standard international (SI) unit of mass is the kilogram (kg).

The concept of mass—the measure of an object’s resistance to acceleration and a key quantity in physics—has evolved for more than two millennia. From early philosophical ideas about matter to the precision of modern particle physics, the understanding of mass reflects humanity’s expanding knowledge of the universe.

Ancient & Classical Concepts

Greek philosophers such as Aristotle (4th century BCE) viewed matter qualitatively, describing the four elements and their natural motions. The idea of “weight” was linked to an object’s tendency to move toward its natural place, not to an intrinsic measurable property.

Renaissance Breakthroughs (16th–17th Century)

Galileo Galilei (1564–1642) separated the ideas of mass and weight by showing that objects fall at the same rate regardless of their heaviness, laying experimental groundwork for an inertial concept of mass.

Newtonian Mechanics (17th Century)

In his Principia (1687), Isaac Newton defined mass as the quantity of matter, measurable by its density and volume, and distinguished between inertial mass (resistance to acceleration) and gravitational mass (source of gravitational attraction). Experiments later confirmed these two are equivalent.

18th–19th Century Developments

Precision balances, chemical analysis (Lavoisier’s law of conservation of mass), and the atomic theory of Dalton established that total mass is conserved in chemical reactions, deepening the quantitative concept of mass.

Einstein’s Relativity (20th Century)

Albert Einstein revolutionized the concept of mass with his theory of relativity (1905–1915). His famous equation E = mc² revealed that mass and energy are interchangeable, showing that mass can be created from energy and vice versa.

Quantum Field Theory & the Higgs Mechanism (20th–21st Century)

In the Standard Model of particle physics, most fundamental particles acquire mass through interaction with the Higgs field. The 2012 discovery of the Higgs boson at CERN’s Large Hadron Collider confirmed this mechanism, completing the modern picture of how mass arises at the subatomic level.

Current Frontiers

Physicists continue exploring dark matter, neutrino masses, and possible physics beyond the Standard Model to answer why particles have the masses they do and how mass shaped the early universe.
