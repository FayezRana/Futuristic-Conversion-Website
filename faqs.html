Length FAQs

Length is a measure of distance between two points, usually expressed in units like meters or feet.

The SI unit of length is the meter (m).

Divide the value in centimeters by 100. Example: 250 cm = 2.5 m.

Divide the value in meters by 1000. Example: 5000 m = 5 km.

1 inch equals 2.54 centimeters.

1 meter = approximately 3.281 feet.

Length is essential in construction, tailoring, travel, science experiments, and daily activities.

Area FAQs

Area is the amount of space inside a 2-dimensional shape, measured in square units.

The SI unit of area is the square meter (m²).

Multiply length × width. Example: 5 m × 3 m = 15 m².

Use the formula π × r², where r is the radius of the circle.

1 m² = 10,000 cm². Multiply the value in m² by 10,000.

A hectare (ha) is a metric unit equal to 10,000 m², commonly used in land measurement.

Area is used in construction, agriculture, interior design, and everyday tasks like buying carpets or tiles.

Temperature FAQs

The main temperature scales are Celsius (°C), Fahrenheit (°F), and Kelvin (K).

Formula: (°C × 9/5) + 32 = °F. Example: 25°C = 77°F.

Formula: (°F − 32) × 5/9 = °C. Example: 68°F = 20°C.

Add 273.15 to the Celsius temperature. Example: 0°C = 273.15 K.

Absolute zero is 0 K (−273.15°C, −459.67°F). It is the lowest possible temperature.

Kelvin is an absolute thermodynamic scale. It is written as K, without a degree sign.

  • Celsius – daily weather, science (worldwide)
  • Fahrenheit – mainly in the U.S.
  • Kelvin – scientific research and physics

Pressure FAQs

Pressure is the force applied per unit area. Formula: P = F / A.

Common units are Pascals (Pa), atmospheres (atm), bar, and mmHg.

Atmospheric pressure is measured using a barometer, often in units of mmHg or hPa.

1 atm = 101,325 Pa. Multiply atm value by 101,325 to get Pascals.

At higher altitudes, atmospheric pressure decreases, reducing the density of oxygen molecules in the air.

Gauge pressure is measured relative to atmospheric pressure. Absolute pressure includes atmospheric pressure as well.

Pressure is used in hydraulics, medicine (blood pressure), aviation, and weather forecasting.

Currency FAQs

Currency is the system of money in use for a country or region, like USD (U.S. Dollar) or PKR (Pakistani Rupee).

Exchange rates show how much one currency is worth in terms of another, e.g., 1 USD ≈ 280 PKR.

Currency values depend on inflation, interest rates, economic stability, and global trade.

Currency can be converted using banks, exchange services, or online converters like XE or Google.

Cryptocurrency is a digital currency like Bitcoin or Ethereum, secured with blockchain technology.

As of now, the Kuwaiti Dinar (KWD) is considered the world’s strongest currency.

Exchange rates fluctuate due to supply and demand, political events, and economic news.

Energy FAQs

Energy is the capacity to do work. It exists in forms like kinetic, potential, thermal, electrical, and chemical.

The SI unit of energy is the Joule (J), defined as 1 Newton meter (N·m).

Renewable energy comes from natural sources like solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal power.

Non-renewable sources include coal, oil, natural gas, and nuclear fuels, which are limited and deplete over time.

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. Formula: KE = ½ mv², where m = mass and v = velocity.

Potential energy is stored energy due to an object's position, like a raised weight or a compressed spring.

Energy conservation reduces costs, saves resources, and helps protect the environment from pollution and climate change.

Speed FAQs

Speed is the distance traveled per unit time. Formula: Speed = Distance ÷ Time.

The SI unit of speed is meters per second (m/s).

Use the formula: m/s = km/h ÷ 3.6. Example: 72 km/h = 20 m/s.

Use the formula: km/h = m/s × 3.6. Example: 10 m/s = 36 km/h.

Average speed is the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken.

Instantaneous speed is the speed of an object at a particular moment in time.

Speed is a scalar quantity (magnitude only), while velocity is a vector quantity (magnitude + direction).

Volume FAQs

Volume is the amount of space that a substance or object occupies, measured in cubic units.

Common units include liters (L), milliliters (mL), cubic meters (m³), and cubic centimeters (cm³).

Divide the number of milliliters by 1000. Example: 500 mL = 0.5 L.

1 liter = 0.001 cubic meters (m³). Example: 2000 L = 2 m³.

Volume = side³ Example: side = 3 m → Volume = 3³ = 27 m³.

Volume = π × radius² × height Example: r = 2 m, h = 5 m → V ≈ 62.83 m³.

Volume is essential in science, engineering, cooking, and daily life for measuring liquids, solids, and gases accurately.

Time FAQs

Time is a measurable period during which events occur, often quantified in seconds, minutes, and hours.

Common units include seconds (s), minutes (min), hours (h), days, and years.

There are 60 seconds in a minute.

There are 60 minutes in an hour.

A leap year has 366 days instead of 365, adding one extra day in February (29th) to align with Earth's orbit.

AM (ante meridiem) refers to the time from midnight to noon, while PM (post meridiem) refers to noon to midnight.

Understanding time helps us schedule events, measure durations, plan activities, and synchronize with others in daily life and science.
