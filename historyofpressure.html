What Is Pressure?

In physics, pressure is defined as the amount of force applied perpendicular to a surface per unit area. It's a scalar quantity, represented by the formula P = F/A, where 'P' is pressure, 'F' is the force, and 'A' is the area over which the force is distributed. The SI unit for pressure is the pascal (Pa) (1 N/m²). Key Aspects of Pressure:

Pressure, defined as force per unit area, is central to physics, engineering, and meteorology. Its understanding evolved from ancient notions of air and fluids to the sophisticated theories of modern science.

Ancient & Classical Ideas

Greek philosophers such as Aristotle and scientists like Archimedes (3rd century BCE) explored air and water behavior. Archimedes’ principle of buoyancy hinted at pressure differences in fluids, though a formal definition of pressure did not yet exist.

Renaissance Experiments (16th–17th Century)

In the early 1600s, engineers puzzled over why suction pumps failed above ~10 meters. Evangelista Torricelli (1643) invented the mercury barometer, proving that air has weight and exerts pressure.

Blaise Pascal extended these studies, demonstrating that atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude and formulating Pascal’s law, which states that pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted undiminished.

18th–19th Century Advances

Daniel Bernoulli (1738) connected pressure with fluid speed in Hydrodynamica, leading to the famous Bernoulli principle.

Later, Joseph-Louis Gay-Lussac, Robert Boyle, and others established gas laws relating pressure, temperature, and volume, laying the foundation for thermodynamics.

20th Century & Modern Physics

Advances in fluid dynamics, meteorology, and material science refined the concept of pressure. High-pressure physics explored matter under extreme conditions, revealing new states like supercritical fluids.

Modern applications range from aerospace engineering and deep-sea exploration to medical devices and semiconductor manufacturing.

Current Frontiers

Contemporary research investigates ultra-high pressures inside planetary cores, pressures in plasma physics, and quantum effects in confined fluids, continuing the long journey of understanding how forces act over area throughout the universe.
